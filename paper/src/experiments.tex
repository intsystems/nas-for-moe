\section{Experiments}
\label{sec:experiments}

We evaluate the proposed framework on two complementary problems:
(i) a controlled 2D toy task that allows us to verify whether the
recovered routing matches the ground-truth cluster structure, and
(ii) the CIFAR-100 image classification benchmark, where we compare
against several MoE and NAS baselines.

\subsection{Toy Experiment}
\label{subsec:toy}

We sample $2000$ points in $\mathbb{R}^2$ from a four-class problem
consisting of one ``linear'' cloud (two classes separated by a
diagonal) and one ``ring'' cloud (two classes corresponding to an
inner and an outer ring). The data is split $80/20$ into
training/validation and clustered into $M = 20$ KMeans clusters,
yielding the partition described in our data preparation pipeline.
We use $K = 2$ experts so that the ideal solution is unambiguous: one
expert should specialise to the linear cloud and the other to the
ring cloud. The search space is the cell-based DARTS-style space of
\cite{liu2018darts}, restricted to small operations suitable for 2D
inputs.

With the parameters reported in our experimental log, SGEM
(Algorithm~\ref{alg:sgem}) recovers the \emph{ideal} cluster split
$(10, 10)$ between the two experts in $20$ EM iterations, matching
the hand-defined oracle assignment that splits clusters according to
the centroid coordinate $x_0 = -2$. This sanity check confirms that
the surrogate-guided objective in~\eqref{eq:moe_objective} is well
posed and that the proposed optimiser solves it when the surrogate
is sufficiently accurate, in agreement with the convergence
guarantee of Theorem~\ref{thm:sgem}.

\subsection{CIFAR-100 and SVHN mixture}
\label{subsec:cifar}

To test whether the cluster-aware objective recovers a meaningful
data partition on realistic data, we build a heterogeneous benchmark
by mixing CIFAR-100 and SVHN in equal proportions ($42\,000$ images
total). SVHN labels are shifted by $100$, so the combined problem has
$110$ classes. The two sources form a maximally separated domain
structure, which gives a well-defined oracle: with $K = 2$ experts the
ideal routing assigns each cluster to the expert specialised on its
source. We partition the data into $M = 30$ \emph{semantic} clusters
obtained from a pretrained feature extractor, using a three-way
train/validation/test split ($0.7/0.15/0.15$). KMeans is fit on the
training embeddings only; validation and test points are assigned to
the nearest centroid. The resulting oracle partition is
$19$ CIFAR-majority and $11$ SVHN-majority clusters, with a mean
source purity of $0.978$.

All MoE configurations use $K = 2$ experts with
\texttt{init\_channels = 16}. The base network is a small DARTS-style
architecture \texttt{CIFAR100Net} (stem, two normal cells, one fixed
reduction cell, global average pooling, fully connected head). The MoE
wrapper instantiates $K$ such networks combined through a fixed hard
\emph{cluster gate}: each cluster is routed to a single expert, so the
gate directly reflects the discovered data partition. Architecture
search uses the train/validation split; the final model is retrained
for $100$ epochs on $\text{train} \cup \text{validation}$ and evaluated
\emph{once} on the held-out test set. We report test accuracy averaged
over several random seeds (the number per method is given in
Table~\ref{tab:cifar_main}). Training is performed inside a Docker
container with PyTorch~2.5.1 on a single RTX~3080~Ti GPU.

\textbf{Load balancing.} Left unregularised, the EM dynamics of
Algorithm~\ref{alg:sgem} tend to collapse all clusters onto a single
expert --- a property of the objective itself rather than of the
optimiser. At a hard routing, where every cluster $m$ is sent to a single
expert $k(m)$ (so $r_{mk} = [\,k = k(m)\,]$), the inner sum
of~\eqref{eq:moe_objective} has a single non-zero term and the logarithm
cancels the exponential, giving
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:hard_objective}
\mathcal{J} \;=\; \sum_{m=1}^{M} |\mathcal{C}_m|\,
\bigl(-u(\boldsymbol{\alpha}_{k(m)}, \mathbf{r}_{k(m)})\bigr)
\;=\; -\sum_{k=1}^{K} N_k\, u_k,
\qquad N_k = \!\!\sum_{m:\,k(m)=k}\!\! |\mathcal{C}_m|,
\end{equation}
with $u_k = u(\boldsymbol{\alpha}_k, \mathbf{r}_k)$ the predicted loss of
expert $k$ and $N_k$ the number of samples routed to it. If the surrogate
becomes nearly constant in its routing argument and the experts are
interchangeable ($u_k \equiv \bar u$), then $\mathcal{J} = -\bar u\, N$ is
independent of the assignment $\{N_k\}$, so the likelihood is flat over
the routing simplex; and even when the $u_k$ differ, $\mathcal{J}$ is
linear in the counts $N_k$ and is maximised by routing all clusters to
the single expert with the smallest $u_k$. To counteract this we augment
the M-step of Algorithm~\ref{alg:sgem} with a load-balancing penalty
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:loadbalance}
\mathrm{LB}(\mathbf{R}) \;=\; K \cdot \sum_{k=1}^{K}\bar{r}_k \cdot p_k,
\qquad
\bar{r}_k = \frac{1}{M}\sum_{m=1}^{M} r_{mk}, \quad
p_k = \frac{|\{m : \arg\max_{k'} r_{mk'} = k\}|}{M},
\end{equation}
weighted by a coefficient $\lambda_{\text{LB}}$; it is minimal for a
uniform routing and saturates at $K$ under full collapse. Because the
correct source partition is itself imbalanced ($19/11$), an aggressive
penalty would push the routing \emph{away} from it; we therefore keep
$\lambda_{\text{LB}}$ small ($\lambda_{\text{LB}} = 1$) and prevent
collapse instead through the data-collection scheme described below.

We compare against the following methods, all evaluated with the same
cluster gate:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Random-MoE}: $200$ random architecture pairs are
    searched at $30$ epochs each on a random cluster split, then the
    best is retrained.
    \item \textbf{DARTS$\times K$}: $K$ experts that share the same
    DARTS-discovered architecture on a random cluster split.
    \item \textbf{SGEM} (ours): Algorithm~\ref{alg:sgem} with a small
    load-balancing weight $\lambda_{\text{LB}} = 1$ and the two
    data-collection fixes described next. No source labels are used at
    any stage.
    \item \textbf{DARTS oracle MoE}: an \emph{upper bound} in which the
    cluster$\to$expert routing is fixed to the true source split and each
    expert uses a DARTS architecture searched on its own source. It uses
    the source labels and is therefore not a competitor but a ceiling on
    what perfect routing can achieve.
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Honest data collection.} An earlier version of our pipeline
reached only $\approx\!0.59$ test accuracy with near-random source
recovery ($\approx\!60\%$), which we traced to two defects in how the
surrogate dataset was built rather than to the EM optimiser. First, each
candidate architecture was evaluated on the \emph{entire} validation set
instead of on the cluster region it was trained for, so an expert trained
on, say, pure-SVHN clusters was scored partly on CIFAR images and looked
poor; this collapsed the predicted-loss gap between a specialised and a
mixed expert and erased the very signal the routing relies on.
Restricting the evaluation to the expert's own clusters
(\emph{region-restricted} surrogate targets) removes the bias. Second,
the cluster masks used to train the surrogate were drawn by including
each cluster independently with probability $\tfrac12$, so a mask
contained $\approx\!M/2$ clusters and a near-pure single-source subset
was essentially never sampled (probability $2^{-19}$ for pure SVHN); the
surrogate therefore never learned that a pure-source expert attains a low
loss. Replacing this with a domain-agnostic \emph{log-uniform} sampler
over the mask size (minimum three clusters) makes small, often near-pure
subsets frequent --- without ever consulting the source labels. With both
fixes and $\lambda_{\text{LB}} = 1$, SGEM recovers the source structure
on its own.

Table~\ref{tab:cifar_main} reports test accuracy with cluster gating.
With the honest data collection, SGEM reaches $0.6199 \pm 0.009$,
outperforming both DARTS$\times K$ ($0.5913 \pm 0.009$) and the random-MoE
baseline ($0.5467 \pm 0.029$) by $+2.9$ and $+7.3$ percentage points and
coming within $1.3$~p.p.\ of the DARTS oracle MoE ($0.6327 \pm 0.004$)
that is \emph{given} the true source split. The cluster gate is what
isolates routing quality: under a learnable gate the methods become
nearly indistinguishable (all $\approx\!0.63$), because a trainable gate
can compensate for a suboptimal partition and so masks the benefit of a
good one.

Because the oracle partition by data source is sharp (mean purity
$0.978$), we can directly check whether the learned routing aligns with
it. Across three seeds the SGEM hard assignment now agrees with the
oracle source split on $92\% \pm 2\%$ of clusters (best of the two label
permutations) --- far above the $\approx\!54\%$ of a balanced random
split and a large jump over the $\approx\!60\%$ of the earlier biased
pipeline. In a typical run (seed $322$, Figure~\ref{fig:svhn_reps}) one
expert becomes a \emph{pure} CIFAR specialist ($17$ clusters, no SVHN)
while the other absorbs all $11$ SVHN clusters together with only $2$
CIFAR ones. The cluster-aware objective therefore \emph{does} discover
the underlying data domains on this benchmark, reversing our earlier
negative finding; the small residual gap to the oracle stems from a few
genuinely mixed clusters and from objective noise near the optimum (the
best-objective iterate occasionally misses the $30/30$ split attained one
iteration earlier).
